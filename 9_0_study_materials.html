Explain Drills Causation · HTS-1
EXPLAIN DRILL 1 OF 2
Explain how technological development after 1900 drove the process of globalization.
EXPLAIN → show HOW or WHY, not just WHAT

Name a specific technology, explain how it worked, and connect it to global integration.

SAMPLE ANSWER — EXPLAIN (Causation)
The development of aviation technology after 1900 drove globalization by dramatically reducing the time and cost of moving people and goods across continents. Before the Wright Brothers’ 1903 flight, intercontinental travel required weeks by sea. Commercial aviation, which expanded rapidly after World War II, compressed those journeys to hours — making international trade, migration, and tourism accessible to millions rather than only the wealthy elite. This physical compression of space accelerated the economic and cultural interconnections that define globalization.
EXPLAIN DRILL 2 OF 2
Explain why globalization produced both cooperation and conflict rather than one or the other.
EXPLAIN → address the tension, not just one side
SAMPLE ANSWER — EXPLAIN (Causation)
Globalization produced both cooperation and conflict because it simultaneously created shared problems that demanded international solutions and generated economic inequalities that sparked resistance. When diseases like the 1918 influenza crossed borders, nations found they could not protect their populations alone — driving the creation of the World Health Organization and international health cooperation. But when trade liberalization through bodies like the WTO transferred manufacturing jobs from wealthy nations to cheaper labor markets, workers and activists organized massive protests, as seen in Seattle in 1999. The same interconnection that made cooperation necessary also made inequality more visible and more contested.

SAMPLE COMPARISONS
Technology — Gain: The World Wide Web (1990) enabled instant global communication and knowledge-sharing, making international scientific collaboration like the Human Genome Project possible across dozens of nations.

Technology — Cost: The same global transportation networks that brought vaccines to remote areas also enabled pathogens like HIV and SARS to spread worldwide far faster than in any previous era.

Economics — Gain: Export-led growth strategies in East Asia allowed millions to rise out of poverty through access to global markets, as seen in South Korea and Taiwan’s rapid industrialization.

Economics — Cost: Multinational corporations could exploit cheaper labor in the Global South, keeping wages low through the threat of relocating production elsewhere.

Culture — Gain: Global media platforms allowed oppressed groups to share their stories and build international solidarity movements, as seen in the Arab Spring’s use of social media.

Culture — Cost: American and Western cultural products dominated global media, threatening indigenous languages, traditional practices, and local artistic traditions.
Apply Activity: The Double-Edged Sword Cross-Unit Connection
APPLY ACTIVITY
The video describes technology in the era of globalization as “a double-edged sword.” Describe one historical example from before Unit 9 that illustrates this same pattern — where a technology or innovation brought both significant benefits and serious costs.
DESCRIBE → provide relevant characteristics, not just a term alone
SAMPLE ANSWER — DESCRIBE (Cross-Unit Connection)
The introduction of the printing press in 15th-century Europe illustrates the double-edged nature of transformative technology. On one hand, the press dramatically accelerated the spread of literacy, scientific knowledge, and religious reform — Martin Luther’s 95 Theses could reach thousands in weeks rather than years. On the other hand, those same capabilities allowed the rapid spread of inflammatory pamphlets, religious propaganda, and nationalist ideas that contributed to decades of violent religious warfare across Europe. The technology was neither inherently good nor bad — its effects depended entirely on who used it and for what purposes.

Continuity & Change Grid CCOT · HTS-3

Unit 9 asks you to identify what was genuinely new after 1900 and what old patterns persisted. Practice both sides.

🔵 Changes After 1900

What was genuinely new in the era of globalization? What had not existed before?

🟠 Continuities After 1900

What old patterns persisted even as the world became more connected?

SAMPLE CCOT ANALYSIS
Changes: The 20th century saw genuinely unprecedented developments. The internet created instant global communication at a scale impossible in any prior era; international organizations like the UN and WTO represented a new attempt to govern above the nation-state level; and the speed at which financial crises, information, and pandemics could spread globally had no historical parallel.

Continuities: Despite these changes, several patterns persisted from earlier periods. Economic inequality between the Global North and South continued older imperial-era hierarchies through new mechanisms like debt dependency and unequal trade terms. Resistance to cultural homogenization echoed earlier responses to imperialism. Great power competition — though now fought through economic and soft-power tools — continued the rivalry dynamics visible in Units 7 and 8.

Contextualization Builder HTS-4 · 3 Pathways

Contextualization connects your topic to a broader historical development. For Unit 9, that means reaching back into Units 7 or 8. Click a pathway to open it, write your own, then read the model response.

PATHWAY 1 · Units 7–8 Connection
World War II and the Institutional Origins of Globalization
▼
Stem: “In the aftermath of World War II, political and economic leaders sought to prevent another catastrophic global conflict by building new international institutions. This context helps explain why…”

Your Contextualization — Pathway 1:

Model Contextualization — Pathway 1
In the aftermath of World War II, political and economic leaders across the victorious Allied powers concluded that the failures of the interwar period — the Great Depression, economic nationalism, and the collapse of international cooperation — had contributed directly to the conditions that made fascism and total war possible. Determined to build a more stable international order, the United States and its allies established new international institutions beginning in 1944–1945: the Bretton Woods system created the International Monetary Fund and World Bank to stabilize currencies and fund reconstruction, while the United Nations (1945) provided a forum for diplomatic conflict resolution. These institutions formed the foundational architecture of the globalized world that Unit 9 examines — they reflect how the trauma of total war pushed states toward cooperation and management of interconnection rather than autarky and rivalry.
PATHWAY 2 · Units 7–8 Connection
Decolonization and the Unequal Terms of Global Integration
▼
Stem: “The wave of decolonization that followed World War II fundamentally shaped the terms on which newly independent nations entered the globalized economy, because…”

Your Contextualization — Pathway 2:

Model Contextualization — Pathway 2
The wave of decolonization that swept Asia, Africa, and the Caribbean between 1945 and the 1970s brought political independence to dozens of new nations — but independence did not erase the economic structures that colonial powers had built over the previous century. Colonial economies had been deliberately organized around raw material extraction and export to the metropole, not domestic industrialization or diversified development. When newly independent states entered the globalized economy of the late 20th century, they largely remained dependent on exporting primary commodities at volatile world prices while importing more expensive manufactured goods — a structural inequality that international institutions like the IMF would later reinforce through structural adjustment programs. This colonial legacy explains why globalization’s economic benefits were distributed so unevenly, generating the resistance and reform movements examined in Topics 9.5 and 9.7.
PATHWAY 3 · Cross-Period Connection
Early Exchange Networks: Silk Roads and the Columbian Exchange
▼
Stem: “The globalization of the 20th century was not the first time networks of exchange had connected the world — earlier examples like the Silk Roads demonstrate that… however, what made post-1900 globalization distinct was…”

Your Contextualization — Pathway 3:

Model Contextualization — Pathway 3
The globalization of the 20th century was not humanity’s first experience with cross-regional exchange. The Silk Roads of the classical and post-classical periods connected East Asia, Central Asia, the Middle East, and Europe in networks of trade, cultural diffusion, and disease transmission — most catastrophically demonstrated when bubonic plague traveled those same routes in the 14th century. The Columbian Exchange after 1492 created genuinely hemispheric biological and agricultural integration with transformative consequences for every region. These earlier networks establish that long-distance connection is a recurring pattern in world history. What distinguished post-1900 globalization, however, was its unprecedented speed, scale, and intentional institutional management: where the Silk Roads emerged organically over centuries of incremental trade, post-WWII globalization was consciously designed through international agreements and organizations, and the digital revolution compressed communication from weeks to milliseconds. This context helps explain why Unit 9 focuses so heavily on institutional responses — the scale and speed of modern globalization demanded governance frameworks that earlier eras never attempted.
SAQ Practice Short Answer Question · 3 Parts · AP Format
Directions: Answer all three parts of the question below. Each part can be answered in approximately 2–3 sentences. You do not need to write an introduction or conclusion. Label each answer (A), (B), (C).
PART A
Describe one way that technological developments after 1900 increased the interconnection of people across the world.
DESCRIBE
SAMPLE ANSWER — PART A
One way technological developments increased global interconnection after 1900 was through the invention and spread of the internet. Tim Berners-Lee’s creation of the World Wide Web in 1990 made the internet publicly accessible, allowing people across the world to share information, conduct commerce, and communicate in real time regardless of geographic distance. This dramatically accelerated the movement of ideas and goods across borders, deepening economic and cultural ties between nations that had previously been limited by physical distance.
PART B
Explain one cause of resistance to economic globalization in the late twentieth century.
EXPLAIN A CAUSE
SAMPLE ANSWER — PART B
One cause of resistance to economic globalization was the perception that institutions like the WTO and IMF disproportionately benefited wealthy nations at the expense of the Global South. IMF structural adjustment programs required developing nations to privatize industries, cut social spending, and open markets in exchange for loans — policies critics argued deepened poverty and eroded national sovereignty. This fueled organized resistance, most visibly in the 1999 Seattle WTO protests, where tens of thousands of demonstrators challenged free-market globalization as economically unjust.
PART C
Explain one way that patterns of economic inequality continued from the colonial era into the period of globalization after 1900.
EXPLAIN A CONTINUITY
SAMPLE ANSWER — PART C
One continuity between the colonial era and the age of globalization was the economic dominance of industrialized nations over developing regions. Just as colonial powers extracted raw materials from colonies while selling finished goods back at a profit, post-colonial structures continued this imbalance. Neo-colonialism — whereby wealthier nations maintained control through debt, trade agreements, and multinational corporations rather than direct political rule — meant formerly colonized nations in Africa, Asia, and Latin America remained economically dependent well into the late twentieth century.
Cross-Unit Synthesis Looking Forward & Backward

🔗 Connecting Unit 9 to the Full Course

Unit 9 is the final chapter of AP World History: Modern — but it doesn’t appear from nowhere. It is the culmination of all the threads you have followed since c. 1200. Consider the connections below:

  • From Units 3–4: The Silk Roads and Indian Ocean trade networks established the pattern of long-distance exchange that globalization scales up dramatically after 1900.
  • From Unit 5: European colonialism built the economic hierarchies (raw-material exporters vs. industrial importers) that persist into globalization’s unequal terms.
  • From Unit 6: Industrial capitalism and its ideological challenges (Marxism, nationalism) created the political economies that globalization both extends and transforms.
  • From Units 7–8: World Wars and the Cold War directly shaped the institutional architecture of post-1945 globalization and determined its winners and losers.

Your Synthesis Task: Choose ONE connection above (or identify your own). Write 3–5 sentences explaining how a development from an earlier unit laid the groundwork for something you expect to study in Unit 9.

SAMPLE SYNTHESIS
European industrialization in the 19th century (Unit 6) directly created the technological and economic foundations for 20th-century globalization. The factory system, steam power, and telegraph networks of the Industrial Revolution established both the infrastructure for global trade and the model of export-oriented economic growth that would define globalization. More importantly, industrialization created the enormous productivity gap between industrialized and non-industrialized nations that persisted into the 20th century — a gap that globalization’s free trade institutions often reinforced rather than closed. The multinational corporations, containerized shipping, and financial markets of Unit 9 are not breaks from industrialization but its fullest global extension.

LEQ PLANNING MODEL
Thesis: “While globalization brought unprecedented technological integration and economic growth after 1900, it transformed the world most significantly in the uneven distribution of its benefits, because the same international institutions that spread free trade and investment also reinforced economic hierarchies that left former colonial nations dependent on wealthier states.”

Contextualization: European colonialism (c. 1450–1900) built global economic hierarchies organized around raw-material extraction in Asia, Africa, and the Americas for the benefit of European industrial economies. These structures did not disappear with decolonization — they were absorbed into the post-WWII globalized economy through unequal trade terms, debt relationships, and the structure of international financial institutions.

Evidence Plan:
1. IMF structural adjustment programs in the 1980s–90s required developing nations to privatize industries and open markets, often deepening poverty — demonstrates unequal terms of globalization.
2. East Asian “Tiger” economies (South Korea, Taiwan) achieved rapid development through state-directed industrial policy, partially contradicting the free-market model — shows complexity.
3. The 1999 WTO protests in Seattle brought together labor unions, environmental groups, and anti-colonial activists — demonstrates organized resistance to globalization’s terms.

Historical Reasoning: CCOT — argument structures around what genuinely changed (speed/scale of integration, institutional architecture) vs. what persisted (north-south economic inequality, resistance to cultural homogenization).
