Recall MCQs — 5 Questions
1. According to the CED, which of the following BEST explains why diseases like malaria and cholera persisted into the late 20th century despite major medical advances?
Correct — B. The video emphasizes: "This isn't a failure of science. It's a failure of access." Diseases of poverty like malaria, TB, and cholera thrive in conditions created by poverty — contaminated water, poor housing, limited healthcare. This directly reflects the CED's ENV theme.
2. The 1918 influenza pandemic killed approximately 50 million people. Which factor most directly explains why it spread so rapidly across the globe?
Correct — C. The video states the flu "spread on trains and steamships... carried across oceans by soldiers and civilians moving for the war." This connects Topic 9.2 to 9.1 (technology) — modern transportation turned a regional outbreak into a global catastrophe.
3. The first successful human heart transplant in 1967 was most directly a response to which of the following trends?
Correct — C. This is the "diseases of longevity" paradox: as people stopped dying from infectious diseases in their 30s–40s, they lived into their 70s–80s and developed age-related conditions like heart disease. Medical success created a new medical frontier.
4. In 1979, the World Health Organization declared smallpox eradicated. This was most significant because it demonstrated:
Correct — B. Smallpox eradication required international coordination through the WHO — a global effort over decades. It represents humanity's single greatest public health achievement and illustrates the power of international institutions (connecting to Topic 9.8).
5. Which of the following pairs CORRECTLY matches a disease category with an example?
Correct — D. All three CED categories correctly matched: TB = disease of poverty (persists due to poor conditions); HIV/AIDS = emergent epidemic (new, global threat); Heart disease = disease of longevity (increased because people live longer). This is the three-column framework you must know.

Explain Drill 1 of 2 — Causation
EXPLAIN How did modern transportation technology contribute to the rapid global spread of the 1918 influenza pandemic?

Remember: Explain means you must show the mechanism — how one thing caused another. Don't just state what happened.

Sample Answer — Explain Drill 1
Modern transportation technology contributed to the rapid global spread of the 1918 influenza pandemic because it dramatically compressed the time and distance that the virus needed to travel between populations. During World War I, millions of soldiers and civilian workers were transported across continents and oceans by train and steamship, creating densely packed, poorly ventilated environments ideal for transmission. A disease that might have remained regional in an era of slower travel instead hitched rides on military supply lines and troop ships, crossing from Europe to North America, Africa, and Asia within months. The 1918 flu thus demonstrates how the same technologies that accelerated industrial warfare also created superhighways for pathogens — a pattern the video connects directly to the Black Death on the Silk Road, but accelerated to the speed of steam.
Explain Drill 2 of 2 — The Paradox of Medical Progress
EXPLAIN Explain why extending the human lifespan through medical advances created new health challenges in the 20th and 21st centuries.

The AP exam loves this paradox. Your answer must explain the mechanism — not just name heart disease or Alzheimer's.

Sample Answer — Explain Drill 2
Medical advances in the 20th century — vaccines, antibiotics, improved surgery, and public health campaigns — dramatically reduced deaths from infectious diseases that had historically killed people in their 30s and 40s. Because people now routinely survived into their 70s, 80s, and beyond, age-related biological processes that were previously cut short by infection now had time to fully develop. Heart disease, stroke, and Alzheimer's disease all become significantly more likely the longer a person lives. This created an entirely new medical frontier: the diseases of longevity. The 1967 heart transplant by Christiaan Barnard and ongoing Alzheimer's research are both direct responses to this phenomenon. In this way, medical success became the engine for new medical problems — a central paradox of the modern era that the video captures as "our single greatest success [creating] a whole new set of medical challenges."

Cross-Unit Connection — 9.2 and 9.1 (Technology)
Connection to Topic 9.1

In 2–3 sentences, explain the specific mechanism connecting the transportation technology of Topic 9.1 to the epidemic disease spread of Topic 9.2.

Sample — Cross-Unit Connection
The railroad and steamship technologies that industrialized nations developed to move goods, troops, and workers at unprecedented speed (Topic 9.1) simultaneously created the conditions for the 1918 influenza to become a global catastrophe (Topic 9.2). When millions of WWI soldiers — many carrying the virus — were transported across continents and oceans on densely packed trains and ships, the pathogen spread from isolated outbreaks to multiple continents within weeks. Without steam-powered transportation, the 1918 flu would likely have remained a regional epidemic rather than killing 50 million people worldwide. Technology was both humanity's greatest asset and its most dangerous vulnerability.

Contextualization Builder — 3 Pathways

Contextualization requires you to connect your topic to broader historical developments before, during, or after the time frame. Each pathway gives you a different angle to practice. Write a contextualization paragraph, then reveal the model.

Pathway 1 — Pre-1900 Disease Patterns as Context
▾
Contextualization stem: Consider how disease spread in the pre-modern era (e.g., the Black Death on the Silk Road, Columbian Exchange diseases) to frame the continuity and acceleration of disease spread in the modern period.

Your Contextualization — Pathway 1:

Model Contextualization — Pathway 1
Throughout human history, the expansion of trade and transportation networks has consistently accelerated the spread of epidemic disease across populations with no prior immunity. The Silk Road facilitated the westward spread of the Black Death in the 14th century, which killed an estimated 30–60% of Europe's population. The Columbian Exchange of the late 15th and early 16th centuries carried smallpox, measles, and influenza to the Americas, decimating Indigenous populations by up to 90% in some regions. These earlier patterns establish a fundamental continuity: human connectivity and disease vulnerability are inseparable. The 20th century did not create this dynamic — it dramatically accelerated it. The steam-powered transportation networks of the industrial era compressed months of travel into days, transforming what might have been regional outbreaks into global pandemics, as demonstrated by the catastrophic 1918 influenza epidemic that exploited WWI-era transportation infrastructure to kill approximately 50 million people worldwide.
Pathway 2 — Cold War Era Global Health Infrastructure
▾
Contextualization stem: Consider how the post-WWII era (Topic 8) created new international institutions like the WHO (founded 1948) that shaped global responses to disease — including the successful smallpox eradication campaign.

Your Contextualization — Pathway 2:

Model Contextualization — Pathway 2
The devastation of World War II and the subsequent Cold War competition created conditions for the emergence of new international institutions aimed at preventing future global catastrophes. In 1948, the newly formed United Nations established the World Health Organization (WHO) as part of a broader effort to build multilateral cooperation on shared global challenges. The Cold War paradoxically enabled international health cooperation even as it divided the world politically — both the United States and Soviet Union participated in the WHO's smallpox eradication campaign, recognizing that epidemic disease respected no ideological border. This institutional architecture, built in the postwar decades, proved decisive when the smallpox campaign successfully eradicated the disease in 1979. The existence of this global public health infrastructure also shaped responses to later crises: the HIV/AIDS epidemic of the 1980s–90s and the 2014 Ebola outbreak both mobilized international responses that would have been impossible without the networks and norms established during the Cold War era.
Pathway 3 — Industrialization and Environmental Change
▾
Contextualization stem: Consider how industrialization and urbanization created dense urban environments where diseases of poverty could thrive — connecting to the AP theme of humans reshaping their environments with unintended consequences.

Your Contextualization — Pathway 3:

Model Contextualization — Pathway 3
The Industrial Revolution of the 18th and 19th centuries fundamentally transformed the human environment in ways that directly shaped disease patterns into the 20th century and beyond. Rapid urbanization drew millions of workers into densely packed cities with inadequate sanitation, contaminated water supplies, and poor housing — conditions perfectly suited to the spread of cholera, tuberculosis, and typhoid fever. These "diseases of poverty" emerged not from poverty alone, but from the specific environments poverty created: overcrowded tenements, shared water sources, and proximity to industrial waste. As the 20th century began, these conditions persisted and spread to newly industrializing regions in Asia, Africa, and Latin America. Even as wealthy industrialized nations eventually improved urban sanitation and living standards, the populations in lower-income countries continued to face the same environmental disease vectors that 19th-century London and Manchester had confronted. This context helps explain the CED's observation that diseases associated with poverty persisted well into the late 20th century — not because science lacked solutions, but because the environmental conditions that enabled them remained largely unchanged for billions of people.

Part B — EXPLAIN

Sample — Part B
The passage reflects the central paradox of modern disease history because the same technologies that enabled disease spread also generated the tools to combat it. The 1918 flu spread via the transportation networks of WWI, killing ~50 million people; yet those same communication and logistical networks later enabled the coordinated WHO campaign that eradicated smallpox in 1979. More recently, the internet — which connects the world and therefore could theoretically accelerate pandemic spread — allows scientists to share viral genetic data globally within seconds, enabling faster vaccine development. Technology is simultaneously the threat vector and the immune system.

Explain ONE way in which environmental factors — including human-created environments — shaped a disease pattern between 1900 and the present.
Sample — Part C
Poverty created environments that shaped the persistence of diseases like tuberculosis throughout the 20th century. Densely packed urban slums with inadequate ventilation, malnutrition weakening immune systems, and limited access to antibiotics created conditions where TB — which spreads through the air — could thrive. This reflects the AP ENV theme: human-created environments of inequality, not natural geography alone, determined which populations suffered the highest disease burden. Even as wealthy nations dramatically reduced TB rates through improved housing and healthcare access, the disease continued killing millions annually in low-income countries where these environmental conditions persisted.
