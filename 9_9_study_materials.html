Argumentation Vocabulary — Flip Cards

Click each card to reveal the definition. These are the key terms and concepts for building complex AP arguments.

Corroborateclick to flip
To back up a claim with multiple, independent pieces of evidence. Corroborating evidence strengthens an argument's credibility.
Qualifyclick to flip
To acknowledge limits, exceptions, or counterarguments to a claim. Signal words: "however," "on the other hand," "while." The secret sauce for AP complexity.
Modifyclick to flip
To adjust or refine an argument in response to new or contradictory evidence. Shows intellectual flexibility and sophistication.
Line of Reasoningclick to flip
The logical framework connecting a thesis to its evidence. A thesis must establish a line of reasoning — not just a fact, but a WHY or HOW.
TCERCclick to flip
AP essay quality checklist: Thesis · Context · Evidence · Reasoning · Complexity. "The Clearest Essays Require Care."
Contextualizationclick to flip
Connecting the topic to broader historical developments BEFORE, DURING, or AFTER the time period. Must be more than a phrase — requires a developed explanation.
Evaluate the Extentclick to flip
A command verb requiring a judgment about DEGREE — how much, for whom, in what contexts. Cannot be answered with a simple yes/no.
CCOTclick to flip
Continuity and Change Over Time — a historical thinking skill. Asks what genuinely changed and what old patterns merely appeared in new forms.
HIPP Analysisclick to flip
A framework for analyzing primary sources: Historical situation · Intended audience · Point of view · Purpose. Required for DBQ sourcing points.
Complexity Pointclick to flip
The hardest AP rubric point to earn. Requires nuance: explaining multiple variables, cross-period connections, insightful comparisons, or effective counterarguments.

Explain Drill 1 of 2
How does technology function as evidence for globalization?

Prompt: Explain how technological developments after 1900 accelerated globalization. Your answer must name at least TWO specific technologies and describe the mechanism by which each one connected the world — not just what it is, but how it functioned as a force of globalization.

Model Explain Response — Drill 1
Technological developments after 1900 dramatically accelerated globalization by shrinking the friction of distance. The internet and smartphones created a near-instantaneous global communication network, allowing activists like those in the Arab Spring to coordinate mass protests across borders in ways that would have been impossible a generation earlier. Jet travel similarly compressed physical distance, making it economically viable for multinational corporations to operate supply chains across multiple continents — extracting raw materials in one region, manufacturing in another, and selling to consumers in a third. In both cases, the mechanism is the same: technology eliminated the barriers of time and distance that previously limited the scope and speed of global connection.
Explain Drill 2 of 2
How did globalization produce both progress and inequality simultaneously?

Prompt: Explain how the same forces of economic globalization produced both new prosperity AND increased inequality after 1900. This is a QUALIFY task — your explanation must use "however" or "on the other hand" to acknowledge both sides and show how they are connected.

Model Explain Response — Drill 2
The free market economic policies championed by Reagan and Thatcher in the 1980s helped integrate the global economy and contributed to the explosive rise of new economic centers in Asia, as Japan, South Korea, and later China industrialized and lifted millions out of poverty. However, these same policies also concentrated wealth among multinational corporations and contributed to what critics called "brain drain" — the flow of educated workers and capital from developing nations to wealthier ones, deepening the dependency of poorer countries. On the other hand, this inequality itself became a catalyst for reform movements: the large-scale protests against the IMF and WTO in the 1990s emerged directly because populations in developing nations experienced free trade as exploitative rather than beneficial. The force and the resistance to that force were products of the same process.

CCOT Grid — Globalization After 1900

A key analytical move in 9.9 is distinguishing what was genuinely new from what were old patterns in new forms. Study this grid — it maps the historical thinking you need for contextualization and CCOT arguments.

🔴 Changes (genuinely new after 1900)
  • Speed of communication: telephone → internet → smartphone in one century
  • Scale of cultural exchange: non-elite populations worldwide participate for the first time
  • Institutional management of global economy (WTO, IMF, World Bank, UN)
  • Green Revolution: global food production transformed through science
  • Mass air travel compressing physical distance for commerce and migration
  • Rights movements gaining global visibility through mass media and social media
🔵 Continuities (old patterns, new forms)
  • Economic inequality between wealthy & developing nations (echoes of imperialism)
  • Competition for resources (oil replacing earlier resource rivalries)
  • Resistance to outside cultural influence (new technologies, same impulse)
  • Powerful nations dominating weaker ones economically (neo-colonialism)
  • Elite-first benefits: global connections existed in 1914 but only for the wealthy
  • Disease crossing borders with trade (COVID ↔ Black Death as pattern)

Analysis question: Using the CCOT grid above, write 2-3 sentences explaining why it would be historically inaccurate to argue that globalization "created" inequality after 1900. What does continuity tell us about the argument?

Model Analysis — CCOT
To argue that globalization "created" inequality after 1900 would ignore centuries of historical precedent. Economic inequality between wealthy and developing nations existed long before the 20th century — it was a defining feature of European imperialism, the Atlantic slave trade, and mercantilist economic systems dating back to the 1500s. What globalization after 1900 actually did was accelerate and intensify pre-existing patterns of unequal exchange, particularly as multinational corporations stepped into roles previously played by colonial governments. The continuity here strengthens a nuanced argument: globalization did not invent exploitation, it modernized it.

Contextualization Builder — Three Pathways

Contextualization requires a developed paragraph — not just a mention — connecting globalization to a broader historical pattern. Three valid pathways are below. Open one, write your paragraph, then check the model.

Pathway 1: Connect to Industrial Revolution & imperialism (pre-1900 origins of global economic inequality) ▼
Stem: "In order to understand the uneven benefits of globalization after 1900, it is necessary to consider the century of industrial capitalism and European imperialism that preceded it. By 1900, the global economy was already structured to benefit industrialized nations at the expense of colonized peoples..."
Your Contextualization — Pathway 1:
Model Contextualization — Pathway 1
In order to understand the uneven benefits of globalization after 1900, it is necessary to examine the century of industrial capitalism and European imperialism that had already structured the global economy by that point. The Industrial Revolution, beginning in Britain in the late 18th century and spreading to Western Europe and North America by the mid-19th century, gave industrialized nations enormous technological and military advantages over non-industrialized societies. European imperial powers exploited these advantages to colonize much of Africa, Asia, and parts of Latin America, integrating these regions into the global economy primarily as suppliers of raw materials and consumers of manufactured goods rather than as equal participants in trade. By 1900, the world was already profoundly interconnected, but interconnected in ways that overwhelmingly benefited European powers. The economic globalization of the 20th century therefore did not create a level playing field — it inherited and, in many ways, amplified the structural inequalities that imperialism had built.
Pathway 2: Connect to Cold War (ideological competition that shaped post-1945 globalization) ▼
Stem: "The nature of post-WWII globalization cannot be understood apart from the Cold War ideological competition between the United States and the Soviet Union that shaped which economic models, institutions, and alliances would govern the newly integrated world..."
Your Contextualization — Pathway 2:
Model Contextualization — Pathway 2
The shape of 20th-century globalization was fundamentally determined by the Cold War rivalry between the United States and the Soviet Union that dominated international relations from 1947 to 1991. The United States championed a capitalist, free-market model of global integration, funding institutions like the World Bank and IMF to spread American-aligned economic frameworks and supporting non-communist governments, even authoritarian ones, in Asia, Latin America, and Africa. The Soviet Union, by contrast, offered an alternative model of state-directed industrialization and economic integration through COMECON, its economic alliance of Eastern bloc nations. This ideological competition meant that globalization after 1945 was never purely economic — it was also geopolitical, with developing nations often forced to choose sides or navigate between competing powers. When the Cold War ended in 1991 and the Soviet model collapsed, the result was an accelerated and largely unchallenged spread of free-market globalization in the 1990s, directly producing both the economic integration and the backlash movements that define the period.
Pathway 3: Connect to World Wars (how global conflict accelerated and redirected globalization) ▼
Stem: "The two World Wars of the 20th century profoundly disrupted existing patterns of global economic integration but also created the conditions for a new, more institutionally managed form of globalization to emerge after 1945..."
Your Contextualization — Pathway 3:
Model Contextualization — Pathway 3
The two World Wars of the 20th century paradoxically both disrupted and ultimately accelerated globalization. World War I fractured the relatively open international trading system that had developed in the 19th century, ushering in an era of economic nationalism, protective tariffs, and the Great Depression, which further contracted global trade in the 1930s. World War II was even more destructive but also more transformative: the massive cost of rebuilding shattered economies created powerful political incentives among Allied powers to design a new international economic framework that would prevent the economic instability they believed had contributed to fascism and the war itself. The Bretton Woods agreements of 1944 established the dollar as the world's reserve currency, created the World Bank and IMF, and laid the institutional groundwork for the free trade order that would define globalization for the rest of the century. In this sense, the destruction of the World Wars was a direct precondition for the global institutional architecture that made post-1945 economic integration possible.
Activity 2 of 4 — LEQ Workshop
Build Your Long Essay Response — Stage by Stage
LEQ Prompt

"Evaluate the extent to which globalization transformed human societies in the period from 1900 to the present."

AP Rubric: Thesis (1pt) + Contextualization (1pt) + Evidence (2pt) + Analysis & Reasoning (2pt) = 6 points total

Stage 1 — Thesis (1 point)
Write a thesis that: (a) makes a historically defensible claim, (b) establishes a line of reasoning (not just a fact), and (c) addresses DEGREE — "to what extent." Use qualifying language ("while," "although," "however").
Model LEQ Thesis
Although globalization after 1900 dramatically transformed the pace and scale of economic integration, cultural exchange, and reform movements worldwide, it did so unevenly — generating transformative prosperity for some nations and populations while reinforcing and accelerating pre-existing patterns of inequality and resource competition for others. The extent of transformation, therefore, depended fundamentally on one's position in the global economic hierarchy, making globalization a force of simultaneous liberation and exploitation.
Stage 2 — Contextualization (1 point)
Choose ONE of your three pathways from the Contextualization Builder above and write your full paragraph here. Remember: a mention is NOT enough — you must develop the connection.
Stage 3 — Body Paragraphs (2 points evidence + 2 points analysis)
Write TWO body paragraphs. Each must: (1) open with a topic sentence tied to your thesis, (2) include at least one specific piece of evidence, and (3) use historical reasoning (causation, comparison, or CCOT) to connect evidence to argument. Paragraph 2 should QUALIFY your argument — use "however" to introduce a complication.

BODY PARAGRAPH 1 — Supports your main argument

BODY PARAGRAPH 2 — Qualifies / Complicates (use 'however' or 'on the other hand')

Model LEQ Body Paragraphs
Body Paragraph 1: Globalization most profoundly transformed the global economy by redistributing economic power away from exclusively Western nations and toward new centers in Asia, fundamentally altering the landscape of global development. The free market policies championed by Reagan and Thatcher in the 1980s helped accelerate trade liberalization, which, combined with deliberate state-led industrialization strategies in East Asia, produced the explosive rise of Japan, South Korea, Taiwan, and eventually China as major economic powers. By the early 21st century, China had become the world's second-largest economy, and hundreds of millions of Chinese citizens had escaped extreme poverty — a transformation of historic proportions that would have been difficult to imagine in 1900. This economic transformation demonstrates that globalization could generate genuinely new outcomes, not merely reproduce old patterns, at least for nations that were positioned to benefit from open markets.

Body Paragraph 2: However, the same economic forces that powered Asia's rise simultaneously entrenched inequality for many nations in Africa and Latin America, suggesting that globalization's transformative power was deeply conditional rather than universal. The structural adjustment programs mandated by the IMF as conditions for loans required developing nations to privatize public industries, cut social spending, and open their markets to foreign competition — policies that, in many cases, produced short-term economic disruption and long-term dependency on foreign capital rather than self-sustaining development. This exploitation was not structurally new: it echoed the extractive logic of 19th-century imperialism, where periphery nations supplied raw materials while wealthy industrial nations captured the value of manufacturing. The large-scale anti-IMF and anti-WTO protests of the 1990s and early 2000s, drawing participants from across the political spectrum and from both wealthy and developing nations, represented a direct repudiation of the claim that free trade was universally beneficial — and thereby qualified the extent to which globalization should be understood as "transformation" rather than intensification of existing hierarchies.
Stage 4 — Complexity (bonus / hardest point)
The complexity point requires more than a good essay — it requires demonstrating sophisticated historical understanding. Three reliable strategies: (1) a cross-period connection, (2) explain how different groups experienced globalization differently, or (3) argue both sides explicitly and explain the contradiction. Choose ONE and write 2-4 sentences to add to your conclusion.
Model Complexity Statement
[Strategy: Cross-period connection + multiple variables] The tension at the heart of globalization — that the same forces of interconnection that generate prosperity also generate exploitation — is not unique to the 20th and 21st centuries. The Silk Road trade networks of the classical era, the Atlantic trading system of the 16th–18th centuries, and the industrialized imperial order of the 19th century all exhibited the same fundamental pattern: the benefits of global exchange flowed disproportionately to those who controlled the terms of trade, while those on the periphery found themselves integrated into a system they had little power to shape. What makes post-1900 globalization historically significant is not that it created this dynamic, but that it made it visible to, and contested by, a far larger portion of the world's population than ever before — suggesting that the truly transformative development may not be globalization itself, but the democratic and rights-based movements that arose in response to it.

Applying Globalization Arguments to the DBQ
DBQ Key Differences from LEQ

The DBQ uses the same thesis and contextualization skills as the LEQ, but adds three additional requirements: (1) use of at least 3 documents as evidence, (2) HIPP sourcing on at least 2 documents, and (3) at least 1 piece of outside evidence beyond the documents. The complexity point is most reliably earned by sourcing 4+ documents AND showing how documents represent competing perspectives.

Practice prompt: A DBQ might present you with 7 documents including: a speech by a WTO official, an anti-globalization protest flyer, a graph of GDP growth in South Korea, a Nigerian economist's critique of IMF structural adjustment, a quote from Malala Yousafzai on social media and reform, a report on climate change, and a chart of rising life expectancy globally. Model Cross-Unit Synthesis
[Unit 9 → Unit 4 connection] The economic structure of 20th-century globalization closely echoes the mercantilist trading systems of the early modern period (c. 1450–1750), suggesting that what changed was primarily the speed and scale of exchange rather than its fundamental logic. Just as European mercantilist powers organized their colonial empires to extract raw materials from the periphery — silver from the Americas, spices from South and Southeast Asia, enslaved labor from Africa — to enrich the metropolitan core, the multinational corporations and trade frameworks of the 20th century organized global supply chains along similar hierarchical lines: manufacturing cheap goods in low-wage economies while capturing profits in wealthy nations. The most significant genuine change between these periods is the emergence of international institutions like the WTO, IMF, and UN that, at least in theory, subject these relationships to international rules and oversight — and the corresponding rise of global advocacy organizations and protest movements that hold those institutions accountable in ways that colonized peoples in 1600 had no equivalent mechanism to do.
