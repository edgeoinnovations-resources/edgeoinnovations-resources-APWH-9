Veto Powerclick to flip
The ability of any P5 nation to block any UN Security Council resolution. Designed to keep great powers in the UN, but frequently caused Cold War gridlock.
League of Nationsclick to flip
1920 international peace organization created after WWI. Failed because the US never joined and it lacked enforcement power; dissolved after failing to prevent WWII.
Universal Declaration of Human Rightsclick to flip
1948 UN document establishing universal standards for how governments must treat citizens — including rights to justice, dignity, and freedom. Not legally binding.
International Monetary Fund (IMF)click to flip
Bretton Woods institution providing emergency loans to countries in financial crisis — often with conditions requiring economic reforms (structural adjustment).
World Bankclick to flip
International financial institution funding development projects (dams, roads, infrastructure) in developing nations — criticized for attaching policy conditions to loans.
World Trade Organization (WTO)click to flip
International body (successor to GATT, est. 1995) that sets rules for global trade and resolves trade disputes between nations; promotes free trade.
Structural Adjustment Programsclick to flip
IMF/World Bank loan conditions requiring countries to cut government spending, privatize industries, and open markets. Critics say they hurt developing nations.
NATOclick to flip
North Atlantic Treaty Organization (1949) — Western military alliance based on collective defense. If one member is attacked, all members respond.
UN Peacekeeping ("Blue Helmets")click to flip
UN military forces deployed to conflict zones to maintain ceasefires and protect civilians. Surged after the Cold War; criticized for failures in Rwanda and Bosnia.
General Assemblyclick to flip
UN body where all 193 member states have one equal vote. Sets agenda and passes non-binding resolutions; less powerful than the Security Council.
✅ Recall Check — Multiple Choice
1. The League of Nations ultimately failed for which of the following reasons?
Correct — B. The video and textbook both emphasize that the League's fatal flaw was US non-participation. This lesson directly shaped the UN's design, which made great power participation non-negotiable by granting them veto power.
2. What was the purpose of the veto power given to the five permanent members of the UN Security Council?
Correct — C. The veto was a deliberate compromise. Without it, the US or USSR could simply walk out — as the US did with the League. The trade-off: great powers stayed in the UN but gained power to create gridlock.
3. The IMF and World Bank were created primarily to address which problem?
Correct — B. The Great Depression and the economic chaos of the 1930s were seen as key contributors to the rise of fascism and WWII. The Bretton Woods institutions (IMF, World Bank) were designed to prevent such economic collapse from happening again.
4. The Universal Declaration of Human Rights (1948) was historically significant because it:
Correct — C. The UDHR was revolutionary in principle: it declared that human rights were universal, not merely domestic affairs. However, it was not legally binding — nations could (and did) ignore it without automatic consequences.
5. How many member states did the United Nations have at its founding in 1945?
Correct — B. The UN was founded with 51 member states in 1945. By 2019, membership had grown to 193 — nearly every country on earth — reflecting how decolonization dramatically expanded the community of recognized nations.

✍️ Explain Drill 1 of 2
Explain why the founders of the United Nations gave veto power to the five permanent members of the Security Council, and why this decision was both necessary and problematic.

Write 3–5 sentences. Use the word because or therefore to show the mechanism.

Model Response
The founders gave veto power to the five permanent members because they had learned a crucial lesson from the League of Nations: without the participation of major powers, any international organization is doomed. The US never joined the League, which fatally weakened it. Therefore, designers of the UN needed to guarantee that the great powers—especially the US and Soviet Union—would stay in the organization even when they disagreed with its decisions. However, this created a serious structural problem: both the US and USSR could now block any Security Council resolution they opposed, and because these two superpowers were often on opposite sides during the Cold War, the veto produced frequent gridlock that prevented the UN from acting in many crises.
✍️ Explain Drill 2 of 2
Explain why critics argued that IMF and World Bank structural adjustment programs were unfair to developing nations.

Write 3–5 sentences. Include a specific example of what "structural adjustment" required and why critics saw it as harmful.

Model Response
Critics argued that structural adjustment programs were unfair because the loans came with conditions that often hurt the very people they claimed to help. To receive emergency funding, countries had to cut government spending, privatize state-owned companies, and open their economies to foreign competition. Because wealthy nations controlled the IMF and World Bank, critics argued these conditions were designed to benefit rich-country investors and corporations rather than local populations. When governments cut spending to meet loan conditions, public services like healthcare and education often suffered, harming working-class citizens in developing nations. Many critics saw the system as a new form of economic control by wealthy nations over poorer ones.

🌐 Contextualization Builder — 3 Pathways

Pick ONE pathway. Use the stem to write a full contextualization paragraph, then compare to the model.

Pathway 1 — The Shadow of World War II ▼
Stem: Before the UN was created in 1945, the world had just experienced the deadliest conflict in human history. Consider how the scale and nature of WWII created specific conditions that made international institution-building feel both urgent and politically possible for the first time.

Your Contextualization — Pathway 1:

Model Contextualization — Pathway 1
The creation of international institutions after 1945 cannot be understood apart from the catastrophic scale of World War II. The conflict killed an estimated 70–85 million people, including the systematic genocide of six million Jews and millions of others in the Holocaust. The war demonstrated that the absence of effective international governance had enabled fascist regimes to expand unchecked, and that national sovereignty alone was insufficient protection against mass violence. By 1945, the Allied powers—having witnessed two world wars in a single generation—shared a political will to build something fundamentally new, making the creation of the United Nations, the IMF, the World Bank, and the UDHR politically feasible in ways that had never been true before.
Pathway 2 — Economic Crisis and Interwar Instability ▼
Stem: The Great Depression of the 1930s demonstrated how economic collapse could destabilize entire societies and fuel the rise of extremist governments. Consider how this economic context shaped the specific design of post-war institutions like the IMF and World Bank.

Your Contextualization — Pathway 2:

Model Contextualization — Pathway 2
The Bretton Woods financial institutions emerged directly from the economic lessons of the 1930s, when the Great Depression had demonstrated how rapidly economic collapse could produce political catastrophe. As global trade contracted and unemployment soared, economically desperate populations in Germany, Italy, and Japan turned toward authoritarian regimes that promised national restoration. The resulting cycle of competitive devaluations, protectionist tariffs, and financial panics convinced planners that uncoordinated national economic policies were dangerous in an interconnected world. At the 1944 Bretton Woods Conference, representatives from 44 Allied nations therefore designed the IMF to prevent currency crises and the World Bank to fund reconstruction, hoping that economic stability would serve as a foundation for lasting peace.
Pathway 3 — Decolonization and the Expanding World Community ▼
Stem: The UN grew from 51 founding members in 1945 to 193 by 2019. Consider how the wave of decolonization that swept Asia and Africa after WWII transformed the composition and debates of global institutions, and what tensions this created.

Your Contextualization — Pathway 3:

Model Contextualization — Pathway 3
The rapid decolonization of Asia and Africa in the decades following World War II fundamentally transformed global institutions that had originally been designed by and for Western powers. Between 1945 and 1975, dozens of newly independent nations joined the United Nations, shifting the majority in the General Assembly toward developing nations and giving voice to perspectives that had been entirely absent from international governance. These new members used the UN General Assembly to condemn colonialism, demand economic justice, and challenge Western-dominated institutions like the IMF and World Bank, which they argued served wealthy nations' interests at the expense of the developing world. The tension between the institution's democratic General Assembly and its power-weighted Security Council thus reflected a broader global struggle over who had the right to define the rules of international order.
