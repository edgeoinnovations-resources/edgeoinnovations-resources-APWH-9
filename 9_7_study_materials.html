Battle in Seattleclick to flip
1999 mass protest in Seattle, WA that shut down the WTO Ministerial Conference. Over 40,000 demonstrators — environmentalists, union workers, activists — marked the birth of a globally recognized anti-globalization movement.
Structural Adjustment Programsclick to flip
IMF/World Bank conditions attached to loans requiring developing countries to cut social spending (austerity), privatize state industries, and open markets. Critics called them a form of economic colonialism.
Fair Trade Movementclick to flip
An economic alternative to mainstream globalization that ensures farmers and artisans in developing countries receive fair prices, safe conditions, and sustainable practices — using consumer choice as a reform tool.
Weiboclick to flip
Chinese social media platform created after China banned Western platforms like Facebook and Twitter. Represents both state control AND cultural assertion — China defining its own digital space rather than accepting Western tech dominance.
Brexitclick to flip
2016 United Kingdom referendum to leave the European Union. Driven by arguments that EU membership eroded British national sovereignty. A political rejection of deep regional economic and political integration.
Rana Plaza Collapseclick to flip
2013 factory collapse in Bangladesh killing 1,100+ garment workers producing for Western brands. Symbolized the hidden human cost of globalization's race for cheaper goods and unsafe labor conditions in supply chains.
Anti-Globalization Movementclick to flip
A broad coalition of activists, labor unions, environmentalists, and NGOs opposing the negative effects of economic globalization — including inequality, labor exploitation, environmental damage, and loss of national sovereignty.
GATT / WTOclick to flip
GATT (1947) = General Agreement on Tariffs and Trade, designed to lower trade barriers globally. WTO (1995) = its successor with far greater enforcement power. Both became targets of anti-globalization protests.

DOK 2 Goal: Move beyond recall. You must establish and explain with examples — showing how and why, not just what.
💬 Explain Drill 1 of 2

AP Skill: Causation — explaining why resistance emerged

Explain why the economic promises of globalization (through organizations like GATT and the WTO) led to significant resistance movements by the late 1990s.

Write 3–5 sentences. Include a specific example (e.g., Rana Plaza, Battle in Seattle, structural adjustment programs).

Model Response — Drill 1
After World War II, institutions like GATT and later the WTO promised that removing trade barriers would create prosperity for all nations. However, the race for cheaper goods produced a devastating contradiction: while multinational corporations and wealthy consumers benefited, workers in developing countries faced brutal conditions and poverty wages. The 2013 Rana Plaza factory collapse in Bangladesh — killing over 1,100 garment workers producing clothes for Western brands — put a human face on this inequality. By 1999, this growing awareness of globalization's hidden costs drove over 40,000 protesters to shut down the WTO Ministerial Conference in Seattle, arguing that the organization served corporate profits rather than people. The resistance was fueled not by a rejection of trade itself, but by a profound sense that the rules of the global economy were rigged against workers, poor nations, and the environment.
💬 Explain Drill 2 of 2

AP Skill: Comparison — explaining similarity across different resistance forms

Explain how China's creation of Weibo and the United Kingdom's Brexit vote both represent resistance to globalization, even though they used very different methods.

Identify what both have in common (the underlying resistance) AND what makes them different (method, type, context).

Model Response — Drill 2
Both China's creation of Weibo and Brexit share a common core: a rejection of the idea that globalization must mean accepting external control over domestic spaces. In both cases, governments or voters prioritized local autonomy over the benefits of deeper global integration. However, their methods and contexts differed significantly. China's move was cultural resistance — the state banned Western platforms and built a domestic alternative, asserting that it would define its own digital identity rather than let American tech companies dominate Chinese information space. Brexit, by contrast, was political resistance — British voters chose to leave a regional economic and political union, arguing that EU membership gave foreign institutions too much power over British law and borders. One was driven by state authority; the other by a democratic vote. One targeted cultural sovereignty; the other targeted political sovereignty. Together, they illustrate that resistance to globalization took many forms depending on the values and concerns of those doing the resisting.

Synthesis Question:

Both Condit and Hoffa are discussing the same phenomenon — economic globalization. Explain how their different positions (CEO vs. union leader) lead them to opposite conclusions about its effects. What does this contrast tell us about why there is no single story of resistance to globalization?

Model HIPP Responses
Source A (Condit) — Key Points:
H: 1998 — peak of WTO expansion, NAFTA's effects visible, global trade growing rapidly.
I: Shareholders, business leaders, possibly policymakers — people who benefit from frictionless global commerce.
P: As CEO of a major exporter (Boeing), Condit benefits enormously from open markets; he frames resistance as futile ("force of nature") to discourage reform efforts.
P: To normalize globalization and discourage regulation — making the status quo seem inevitable rather than chosen.

Source B (Hoffa) — Key Points:
H: 1999 — just before Seattle protests, American manufacturing jobs declining, NAFTA blamed for outsourcing.
I: Union members and workers whose livelihoods are threatened — to mobilize them for protest and political action.
P: As a union leader, Hoffa represents workers who directly bear the costs of globalization's "race to the bottom" in wages and conditions.
P: To delegitimize the WTO and build support for the anti-globalization movement ahead of Seattle.

Synthesis: Condit and Hoffa's opposing views reflect their material interests — one profits from global markets, one represents those harmed by them. This contrast reveals a central truth: resistance to globalization was not irrational or monolithic. It arose from real, documented harm experienced by specific groups. There is no single story because there is no single experience of globalization.

1 Pathway 1 — Colonial Economic Legacies

Stem: "Resistance to globalization after 1900 was shaped by the long shadow of European imperialism. During the 19th century, colonial powers [describe the economic extraction system]... This legacy of economic exploitation meant that when institutions like the IMF and World Bank imposed structural adjustment programs, many in the developing world [explain the connection to colonial memory]..."

Your Contextualization — Pathway 1:

Model Contextualization — Pathway 1
During the 19th and early 20th centuries, European imperial powers systematically extracted raw materials and wealth from colonized regions in Africa, Asia, and Latin America, integrating these territories into a global economy designed to serve European industrial interests rather than local development. Colonial administrations suppressed local manufacturing, forced cash crop production, and created dependency relationships that left formerly colonized nations structurally disadvantaged when they gained independence in the mid-20th century. This history of economic exploitation gave the anti-globalization movements of the late 20th century a powerful resonance in the developing world: when the IMF and World Bank attached austerity conditions to their loans through structural adjustment programs, activists in countries from Argentina to Ghana drew explicit parallels to colonial extraction — arguing that the language had changed but the power dynamics had not. The resistance to globalization, therefore, was not merely a reaction to contemporary inequality but was shaped by generations of experience with economic systems that promised development while delivering dependency.
2 Pathway 2 — Cold War Ideological Competition
▼
Stem: "The resistance to economic globalization that emerged in the late 20th century built on a longer history of ideological alternatives to free-market capitalism. During the Cold War, [describe how socialist/communist movements offered critiques of capitalism]... When the Soviet Union collapsed in 1991 and capitalism appeared triumphant, [explain how this shaped the anti-globalization movement that followed]..."

Your Contextualization — Pathway 2:

Model Contextualization — Pathway 2
Throughout the Cold War era, the Soviet Union and socialist movements around the world offered a sustained ideological critique of Western capitalism, arguing that free markets produced inequality, exploitation, and imperialism. Labor movements in Western countries, liberation movements in the developing world, and socialist governments from Cuba to Tanzania pursued alternatives to the free-market model championed by the United States. When the Soviet Union collapsed in 1991, many observers declared the "end of history" and the permanent triumph of liberal capitalism and free trade. However, rather than eliminating resistance to capitalism's global expansion, the Cold War's end paradoxically intensified it: with no Soviet alternative to constrain Western financial institutions, the IMF and World Bank pursued aggressive structural adjustment programs across the developing world, and the WTO was established in 1995 with unprecedented enforcement power. The anti-globalization movement that erupted at Seattle in 1999 can thus be understood as a continuation of Cold War-era critiques of capitalism, now directed at the specific institutions managing the post-Cold War economic order.
3 Pathway 3 — Nationalism and the Nation-State
▼
Stem: "Political resistance to globalization — most dramatically illustrated by Brexit — drew on a long tradition of nationalism that prized state sovereignty over international integration. Since the 19th century, [describe the rise of nationalism and its connection to state identity]... The post-WWII project of international integration [through the UN, EU, Bretton Woods institutions] challenged these nationalist traditions, and by the early 21st century [explain how the tension exploded]..."

Your Contextualization — Pathway 3:

Model Contextualization — Pathway 3
Since the French Revolution and the nationalist movements of the 19th century, the nation-state had emerged as the primary framework for political identity and self-determination — the idea that a distinct people should govern their own affairs within defined borders. This tradition held that sovereignty was not to be surrendered to outside authorities, and it powered both the consolidation of European nations and the decolonization movements of the 20th century. After World War II, Western powers built international institutions — the United Nations, the European Economic Community (later the EU), and the Bretton Woods financial system — that deliberately transferred some degree of national authority to supranational bodies, on the theory that cooperation would prevent another catastrophic war. However, as the EU deepened economic and political integration, and as the WTO gained authority to override national regulations, a backlash built among populations who felt that elected governments had ceded control over fundamental decisions about immigration, trade, and law to unaccountable technocrats. Britain's 2016 Brexit vote crystallized this tension: for many voters, "taking back control" from Brussels was not anti-modern but rather a reaffirmation of the 19th-century nationalist principle that legitimate authority must rest with the people of a sovereign nation.
📝 SAQ Practice — Parts A, B, C
SAQ Prompt — No Stimulus (Period 8–9 type)
Answer parts A, B, and C. Answer each part in 2–3 sentences. Specific historical evidence is required. Do not write an introduction or conclusion.
Part A
Identify one specific example of economic resistance to globalization after 1900 and briefly describe what it opposed.

Tip: "Identify" means name it clearly enough that the reader knows exactly what you mean. Then describe what it was resisting — 2 sentences is enough.

Model Response — Part A
One example of economic resistance to globalization was the 1999 "Battle in Seattle," in which over 40,000 protesters shut down the World Trade Organization's Ministerial Conference. These demonstrators — including labor unions, environmental groups, and human rights activists — opposed the WTO's rules, which they argued prioritized corporate profits over workers' rights, environmental protections, and the sovereignty of developing nations.
Part B
Explain how ONE cultural or political form of resistance to globalization after 1900 reflected concerns beyond economic inequality.

Tip: "Explain" means show the how or why. Don't just name the example — connect it to the specific non-economic concern it addressed.

Model Response — Part B
China's decision to ban Western social media platforms like Facebook and Twitter, and to create the domestic alternative Weibo, reflected concerns about cultural sovereignty rather than purely economic grievances. By building its own digital infrastructure, China asserted the right to define its own information space and resist the cultural dominance of American technology companies — a form of resistance rooted in national identity and state authority rather than labor rights or trade rules. This example illustrates that resistance to globalization could be as much about who controls cultural spaces as who controls economic ones.
Part C
Explain how the variety of responses to globalization after 1900 — economic, cultural, and political — reflects the larger argument that globalization created both winners and losers.

Tip: This is the synthesis part. Connect all three types of resistance to the underlying idea about globalization's unequal impact. This earns the "explain" point when you show the mechanism.

Model Response — Part C
The diversity of resistance to globalization — from street protests against the WTO to Brexit to China's digital sovereignty — reflects the fact that globalization's costs were distributed unequally across economic classes, cultures, and nations. Workers in developing countries bore the physical dangers of unsafe factories; local cultures felt overwhelmed by the dominance of Western media and technology; and citizens in nations like the UK perceived that economic integration had transferred democratic power to unaccountable supranational institutions. Each form of resistance, though different in method and target, shared the common conviction that the benefits of a more connected world were being captured by a narrow group of winners while the costs — lost jobs, eroded identities, diminished sovereignty — fell on everyone else.
