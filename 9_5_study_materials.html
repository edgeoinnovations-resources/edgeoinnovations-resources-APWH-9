Recall Check: 5 Multiple Choice Questions

1. The Universal Declaration of Human Rights (1948) was considered radical in its historical context primarily because it
Correct — B. The word "universal" was the radical act. Colonial empires still existed and racial segregation was legally enforced in many nations, making a declaration of universal equality a direct challenge to those systems.
2. Which of the following best describes the Negritude movement's primary strategy?
Correct — C. The Negritude movement "flipped the script" on colonial racism by reclaiming and celebrating Black culture and identity rather than accepting European cultural supremacy — using culture itself as the weapon.
3. The U.S. suffrage victory of 1920 is described in the video as an incomplete victory because
Correct — B. The 1920 victory was largely for white women. African-American women's voting rights were not fully protected until the Voting Rights Act of 1965 — 45 years of continued struggle, demonstrating that progress was "incredibly uneven."
4. What made apartheid in South Africa particularly systematic compared to other forms of racial discrimination?
Correct — C. Apartheid was "a brutal, meticulously designed system" — not just informal racism. It used pass books, forced residential separation, marriage laws, and language mandates to dehumanize 85% of the population under the rule of a tiny white minority.
5. Liberation theology and the Negritude movement were similar in that both
Correct — C. These were very different movements in different places, but they shared a common strategy: taking their own identity (Black culture; Catholic faith) and turning it into a powerful argument for liberation and justice rather than accepting the terms imposed by dominant powers.

Explain Drill 1 of 2 · AP Skill: Causation

Explain why the creation of the United Nations and the Universal Declaration of Human Rights in 1948 represented a significant turning point in global history. Your answer must show the mechanism — how or why this mattered — not just what happened.

Sample Explanation (DOK 2 — Explain with Examples):

The UN and UDHR represented a turning point because they established a universal moral framework at a moment when the world had just witnessed what happens when no such framework exists. After two devastating world wars and the Holocaust, there was collective recognition that without enforceable standards of human dignity, atrocities would continue. The UDHR's power was not just symbolic — it provided the language and the legitimacy for future reform movements around the world to make their case. For example, anti-apartheid activists, global feminists, and caste reform advocates in India could all point to the UDHR as evidence that their demands were not just local grievances but violations of a universally recognized standard. The UN then created agencies like UNICEF and UNHCR to translate those ideals into concrete action.
Explain Drill 2 of 2 · AP Skill: Causation + Comparison

Explain how the dismantling of apartheid in South Africa required both internal resistance and international pressure. What made that combination effective?

Sample Explanation (DOK 2 — Explain with Examples):

Apartheid's collapse required a "one-two punch" because neither force alone was sufficient. Internal resistance — led by figures like Nelson Mandela and the ANC — provided moral legitimacy and demonstrated that a majority of South Africans refused to accept the system as permanent. Without this, international pressure would have had no credible partner to support. International pressure — through economic sanctions, cultural boycotts, and diplomatic isolation — strangled South Africa's economy and removed the regime's ability to sustain itself financially. Without internal resistance, sanctions might have simply hardened the government's resolve. Together, these two forces made the cost of maintaining apartheid unsustainable, leading to the first free elections in 1994. This shows that successful reform movements often depend on an alignment between grassroots pressure from below and structural pressure from outside.

Reflection: What is one similarity and one difference among these three movements?

Similarity: All three movements used their own identity — gender, race, or religious faith — as a tool of empowerment rather than a source of shame, turning what dominant systems had used to marginalize them into the foundation of their argument for justice.

Difference: While global feminism and liberation theology both eventually engaged with formal international institutions (UN conferences, Vatican councils), the Negritude movement was primarily an intellectual and cultural project that worked through literature, poetry, and art rather than legal or political lobbying.

Contextualization Builder — 3 Pathways

The AP exam's highest-value skill is contextualization — placing your topic within a broader historical process. The video explicitly names three contextual connections for 9.5. Open each pathway, write your own contextualization paragraph, then compare to the model.

Pathway 1: Connect to Decolonization (Unit 8)

▼ open

Stem: As colonial empires crumbled after WWII, newly independent nations and cultural movements needed new frameworks for identity and political legitimacy. The Negritude movement and other cultural assertions of pride rose directly in response to colonial dehumanization. This broader process of decolonization created the political and cultural conditions in which 9.5's reform movements could gain global traction.

Your Contextualization — Pathway 1:

Model Contextualization — Pathway 1
The global wave of decolonization that followed World War II fundamentally reshaped the context in which reform movements operated. As European empires dismantled across Asia and Africa through the 1940s–1960s, formerly colonized peoples demanded not merely political independence but cultural recognition — a rejection of the colonial assumption that European civilization represented the pinnacle of human achievement. This created fertile ground for Topic 9.5's reform movements: the Negritude movement, which emerged from French West Africa and the Caribbean, directly challenged the racial and cultural hierarchies that colonialism had imposed, celebrating Black identity at precisely the moment when colonial frameworks were losing their political legitimacy. The global context of decolonization thus provided both an audience and a moral vocabulary for rights-based challenges to race, class, and cultural hierarchy that would define 20th-century reform movements.

Pathway 2: Connect to the Cold War (Units 8)

▼ open

Stem: The Cold War created an unusual dynamic for human rights: both superpowers weaponized human rights discourse, each pointing to the other's abuses as evidence of their system's moral failure. This put a global spotlight on issues of racial segregation (U.S. Jim Crow), political repression (Soviet gulags), and colonial subjugation. Paradoxically, Cold War rivalry both complicated and accelerated reform movements.

Your Contextualization — Pathway 2:

Model Contextualization — Pathway 2
The Cold War rivalry between the United States and the Soviet Union placed human rights at the center of global politics, creating a paradoxical context for the reform movements of Topic 9.5. Both superpowers sought to win the allegiance of newly independent nations in Asia, Africa, and Latin America by presenting their system as the true defender of human dignity. This meant the United States faced intense international pressure over its domestic practice of racial segregation — the Soviet Union consistently highlighted Jim Crow laws as evidence of American hypocrisy. This Cold War spotlight accelerated the Civil Rights movement by giving its demands a geopolitical urgency: maintaining racial apartheid in the American South was becoming an international liability. Similarly, the U.S. supported anti-colonial movements in certain contexts to counter Soviet influence, inadvertently lending legitimacy to the broader language of rights and self-determination that empowered many reform movements of this period.

Pathway 3: Connect to 19th-Century Roots (Units 6–7)

▼ open

Stem: The video emphasizes that 20th-century reform movements "stood on the shoulders of earlier abolitionists and suffragettes." 19th-century abolitionism established the principle that human beings could not be legally owned; suffrage movements established that political participation was a universal right. These earlier struggles created the moral and legal vocabulary that 20th-century movements would inherit and radicalize.

Your Contextualization — Pathway 3:

Model Contextualization — Pathway 3
The reform movements of the 20th century did not emerge from a vacuum but built upon a foundation established by 19th-century abolitionists and suffragettes who had already begun to challenge the legal and social hierarchies that defined the modern world. The abolition of slavery across the Atlantic World — from the Haitian Revolution to the British Slavery Abolition Act (1833) to the U.S. 13th Amendment (1865) — established the foundational principle that human beings possessed inherent dignity that could not be legally negated. Similarly, early women's suffrage movements in New Zealand (1893) and parts of Europe created the precedent that political participation was not inherently male. These earlier victories gave 20th-century reform movements their moral language, their organizational models (mass petition campaigns, international advocacy), and their central claim: that rights once recognized could not logically be withheld from anyone. The global feminism, anti-apartheid, and rights-based movements of Topic 9.5 were therefore not new inventions but radical extensions of a project that had begun a century earlier.
