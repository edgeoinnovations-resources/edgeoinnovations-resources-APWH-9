✅ Recall Check — Multiple Choice

Select the best answer. Wrong answers are eliminated; keep trying until you find the right one.

1. Which of the following best describes "economic liberalization" as it occurred after 1980?
Correct — B. Economic liberalization meant governments "stepped back" — cutting taxes, slashing regulations, privatizing industries, and opening to foreign trade. This was the core policy shift championed by Reagan, Thatcher, Pinochet, and Deng Xiaoping, though in very different political systems.
2. Deng Xiaoping's economic reforms in China were significant primarily because they
Correct — C. Deng Xiaoping's genius was allowing market reforms and foreign investment through Special Economic Zones — fueling explosive economic growth — while the Communist Party maintained iron control over politics. This defied the idea that capitalism and communism couldn't coexist.
3. The growth of maquiladoras in Mexico in the late 20th century is best understood as a result of
Correct — C. After NAFTA removed tariff barriers, US companies could build factories in Mexico (maquiladoras) where wages, taxes, and regulations were lower. Workers assembled goods that were then shipped back to the US for sale — a classic example of global production shifting to lower-wage regions.
4. Which of the following BEST illustrates the difference between a "knowledge economy" and a "manufacturing economy"?
Correct — B. Knowledge economies (US, Finland, Japan) produce ideas, data, and innovation. Manufacturing economies (Vietnam, Bangladesh, Mexico) specialize in assembling and producing physical goods. These two types are interdependent — the design happens in one place, the production in another.
5. When analyzing a source on the AP exam using the "sourcing" skill, a student should consider which of the following?
Correct — D. Sourcing (HIPP) means analyzing Historical situation, Intended audience, Point of view, and Purpose. A politician's speech and an academic report may reach the same conclusion but for very different reasons — and understanding those differences is the essence of high-level historical analysis on the AP exam.

🖊 Explain Drill 1 of 2 — Causation
Prompt: Explain ONE way in which the end of the Cold War caused the acceleration of free-market economic policies in the late 20th century.

📌 Skill tip: "Explain" means you must show the connection — how or why. A good response names a development AND connects it to the effect. Aim for 2–3 sentences.
Model Response
The collapse of the Soviet Union and the end of the Cold War in 1989–1991 discredited communism as a viable global economic alternative, removing the main ideological challenge to free market capitalism. With central planning largely "off the table," governments across Eastern Europe, Latin America, and Asia faced pressure — from international lenders like the IMF and from trading partners — to embrace economic liberalization policies such as privatization, deregulation, and open trade. The decisive failure of the Soviet command economy gave free market advocates like Reagan and Thatcher powerful evidence that markets, not governments, should drive economic growth.
🖊 Explain Drill 2 of 2 — Continuity & Change
Prompt: Explain ONE way in which the global economic divide between wealthier and poorer nations continued even as the global economy became more interconnected after 1980.

📌 CCOT tip: Look for something that seems new on the surface but reflects an older pattern of inequality. Use a specific example to anchor your answer.
Model Response
Even as globalization created new trade networks and jobs in developing nations, the fundamental economic hierarchy between wealthy and poorer nations persisted. Countries like Vietnam, Bangladesh, and Mexico gained manufacturing jobs, but the higher-value activities — design, research, patents, and profits — remained concentrated in developed knowledge economies like the United States, Finland, and Japan. Maquiladoras in Mexico, for example, created employment but were notorious for low wages and poor working conditions, while the profits flowed back to foreign corporations. This mirrored earlier patterns of economic imperialism where peripheral nations supplied labor or raw materials while the core nations captured most of the economic gains.

🌐 Contextualization Builder — 3 Pathways

Write your own contextualization paragraph using one of these stems, then compare it to the model. A strong contextualization paragraph is 3–5 sentences and connects your topic to a broader historical development — not just background facts.

Pathway 1Connect to pre-1980 economic history (earlier globalization waves) ▼
Sentence Stem: "Although the free market globalization of the late 20th century seemed unprecedented, it was part of a longer history of economic integration dating back to..."

Your Contextualization — Pathway 1:

Model Contextualization — Pathway 1
Although the free market globalization of the late 20th century seemed unprecedented, it was part of a longer history of economic integration dating back centuries. European imperialism of the 19th century had already created a global division of labor in which colonies supplied raw materials while industrialized powers produced manufactured goods for export — a structure that profoundly shaped the economic geography of Africa, Asia, and Latin America. The post-1980 globalization era mirrored this pattern in new forms: former colonial regions now supplied low-cost manufacturing labor, while former imperial powers transitioned into knowledge and service economies. Understanding this continuity helps explain why many developing nations and critics viewed late 20th century free trade not as liberation but as a repackaged version of economic dependency.
Pathway 2Connect to Cold War ideology and the fall of communism ▼
Sentence Stem: "The rapid spread of free market economics after 1980 must be understood against the backdrop of the decades-long ideological struggle between capitalism and communism..."

Your Contextualization — Pathway 2:

Model Contextualization — Pathway 2
The rapid spread of free market economics after 1980 must be understood against the backdrop of the decades-long ideological struggle between capitalism and communism that defined the Cold War. For much of the 20th century, Soviet-style central planning represented a credible alternative economic model, and many newly independent nations in Africa, Asia, and Latin America experimented with state-led development or non-alignment. However, by the 1970s and 1980s, communist economies were visibly struggling — stagnant growth, shortages, and technological lag — while capitalist economies in the West surged ahead. When the Soviet Union collapsed in 1991, it effectively removed the main ideological barrier to free market dominance, creating the conditions for the WTO, regional trade blocs, and multinational corporations to reshape the global economy on capitalist terms.
Pathway 3Connect to technological revolution and communications (9.1 link) ▼
Sentence Stem: "The emergence of knowledge economies and global supply chains in the late 20th century would have been impossible without the simultaneous revolution in information and communications technology..."

Your Contextualization — Pathway 3:

Model Contextualization — Pathway 3
The emergence of knowledge economies and global supply chains in the late 20th century would have been impossible without the simultaneous revolution in information and communications technology. The development of the internet, personal computers, and international telecommunications infrastructure in the 1970s–1990s allowed companies to coordinate production across multiple continents in real time — a design team in California could work with a manufacturer in Vietnam and sell the finished product in Germany through a single, seamlessly managed supply chain. This technological infrastructure was the "backbone" of economic globalization, enabling the knowledge economy to flourish in developed nations while connecting manufacturing regions in Asia and Latin America to global markets. Without these technological advances, the MNC-driven model of dispersed production simply could not have existed at the scale it reached by the early 21st century.
