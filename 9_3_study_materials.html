► Recall Check — 5 Multiple Choice Questions
1. Which of the following best describes the primary driver of increased environmental pressure after 1900?
Correct — B. The video and textbook both emphasize that population growth from 1.6 billion to over 6 billion, combined with industrial-era urbanization, drove unprecedented resource demands that stressed forests, land, air, and water systems worldwide.
2. The Aral Sea disaster is used as an example of which environmental problem?
Correct — C. The Aral Sea was once one of the world's largest lakes. Soviet-era irrigation projects diverted the rivers feeding it, causing it to shrink drastically and leave behind a toxic desert — a prime example of desertification driven by human activity.
3. According to the greenhouse gas theory, what is the mechanism by which fossil fuel combustion causes global warming?
Correct — B. The video uses the "thickening blanket" analogy: Earth already has a natural heat-trapping atmosphere. Burning fossil fuels adds more CO₂, making that blanket thicker and trapping more heat — the core mechanism of global warming.
4. Which international agreement, reached in 2015, represented the broadest global commitment to limiting temperature rise?
Correct — C. In 2015, 195 countries signed the Paris Agreement, committing to limit global temperature rise to well below 2°C. It built on the Kyoto Protocol (1997) but involved far more nations. The US later withdrew under President Trump in 2017.
5. The term "Anthropocene" is used by scientists to describe what?
Correct — C. "Anthropocene" literally means "age of humans." Scientists proposed this to reflect that humanity has become the single largest force shaping the planet's climate and environment — for better and for worse.

Show Your Understanding

► Explain Drill 1 of 2 — Causation
Explain how population growth after 1900 contributed to environmental degradation. Your response should show a clear cause-and-effect chain, not just list facts.
Hint: Think population → demand → land use → specific effects (deforestation, desertification, etc.)
Model Response
The rapid growth of the world's population—from 1.6 billion in 1900 to over 6 billion by 2000—created an enormous demand for food, water, and energy that the Earth had never before faced. To feed billions more people, vast areas of forest were cleared for croplands and ranches, particularly in places like the Amazon rainforest, destroying habitats and weakening the planet's natural ability to absorb carbon dioxide. At the same time, the need for more agricultural land in drier regions led to overgrazing and the exhaustion of soil, causing desertification in places like Africa's Sahel region. Industrial cities powered by coal and oil poured pollutants into the air, while agricultural and industrial water demand caused global freshwater consumption to skyrocket after 1950. In this way, population growth set in motion a cascade of environmental pressures across forests, land, air, and water simultaneously.
► Explain Drill 2 of 2 — Multiple Perspectives
Explain why developed and developing nations have often disagreed about responsibility for addressing climate change. Use specific examples or evidence to support your explanation.
Hint: Think about who created the problem historically vs. who is industrializing now. What does each side argue?
Model Response
Developed and developing nations have clashed over climate responsibility because they have fundamentally different historical relationships with industrialization. Wealthy nations like the United States and those in Western Europe spent over 150 years burning fossil fuels to build their economies, creating the bulk of the accumulated greenhouse gases in the atmosphere today. When these nations called on rapidly industrializing countries like China and India to cut emissions, developing nations pushed back, arguing that it was unjust to deny them the same path to economic growth that made the West wealthy. Additionally, developed nations still maintain much higher per-capita emissions than developing ones, strengthening the argument that industrialized countries must lead the transition. This tension is visible in agreements like the Paris Agreement (2015), which acknowledged that developing countries would need more time to reach peak emissions—a compromise reflecting the unequal historical contributions to the problem.

► Contextualization Builder — 3 Pathways

Pathway 1
The Industrial Revolution as Context for Environmental Change
▼
Stem: Beginning in Britain in the late 18th century and spreading globally through the 19th and 20th centuries, the Industrial Revolution transformed how humans produced goods, organized cities, and consumed energy. [Continue describing this broader development, then connect it explicitly to why the environmental debates of the 20th century emerged when and how they did.]

Your Contextualization — Pathway 1:

Model Contextualization — Pathway 1
Beginning in Britain in the late 18th century and spreading globally through the 19th and into the 20th century, the Industrial Revolution fundamentally transformed how humans produced goods, consumed energy, and organized society. Driven by coal-powered steam engines, factories, and railroads, industrializing nations burned fossil fuels on an unprecedented scale and accelerated urbanization, drawing millions away from rural subsistence into dense, energy-hungry cities. By the early 20th century, industrialization had also triggered a population surge as improved medicine and food production supported billions more lives on Earth. These long-term trends—accelerating fossil fuel use, massive urban growth, and population expansion—set the stage for the environmental crises of Topic 9.3, as the cumulative effects of more than a century of industrial activity began to destabilize global climate systems, deplete natural resources, and spark an international debate over how to balance economic development with environmental sustainability.
Pathway 2
Imperialism, Resource Extraction & Environmental Exploitation
▼
Stem: During the 19th century, European powers dramatically expanded their empires across Africa, Asia, and the Americas, driven in large part by the demand for raw materials to fuel industrial economies. [Describe this context further and connect it to how global resource consumption patterns after 1900 contributed to environmental change.]

Your Contextualization — Pathway 2:

Model Contextualization — Pathway 2
During the 19th century, European powers dramatically expanded their empires across Africa, Asia, and the Americas, driven largely by the demand for raw materials to feed industrial economies back home. Colonial powers extracted rubber, timber, minerals, and agricultural products on a massive scale, clearing forests, reorganizing land use, and introducing monoculture farming practices that degraded local ecosystems. The global systems of resource extraction established under imperialism created entrenched patterns of environmental exploitation that continued well into the 20th century, even after formal colonialism ended. These patterns help explain why, in debates over climate change after 1900, formerly colonized and developing nations argued that the wealthiest countries bore the greatest historical responsibility—because it was the industrialized world's centuries-long exploitation of global resources that created the environmental crisis in the first place.
Pathway 3
The Green Revolution & Intensified Agricultural Pressure
▼
Stem: Beginning in the 1940s and accelerating through the 1960s–70s, the Green Revolution used new high-yield crop varieties, synthetic fertilizers, and pesticides to dramatically increase global food production. [Describe this broader context and connect it to why freshwater consumption and land-use pressures accelerated so sharply after 1950.]

Your Contextualization — Pathway 3:

Model Contextualization — Pathway 3
Beginning in the 1940s and accelerating through the 1960s and 1970s, the Green Revolution used new high-yield crop varieties, synthetic fertilizers, and chemical pesticides to dramatically increase global food production, particularly in South Asia and Latin America. While this technological transformation helped avert widespread famine for a rapidly growing global population, it came with significant environmental costs: massive irrigation projects consumed freshwater at unsustainable rates, chemical runoff contaminated rivers and soils, and the expansion of farmland into previously uncultivated areas accelerated deforestation. These dynamics help explain why, as shown in Topic 9.3, global water use skyrocketed particularly after 1950—the very period when Green Revolution agriculture was being most intensively deployed. The environmental pressures created by the effort to feed a post-WWII population boom thus became a major driver of the resource competition and water scarcity debates that define the environmental story of the 20th and 21st centuries.
► SAQ Practice — Short Answer Question (Source-Based)
Excerpts from key articles of the Paris Agreement, 2015:

“The Paris Agreement, in seeking to strengthen the global response to climate change, reaffirms the goal of limiting global temperature increase to well below 2 degrees Celsius, while pursuing efforts to limit the increase to 1.5 degrees.”

“Parties aim to reach global peaking of greenhouse gas emissions as soon as possible, recognizing peaking will take longer for developing country Parties…”

“The Paris Agreement also encourages Parties to conserve and enhance, as appropriate, sinks and reservoirs of [greenhouse gases], including forests.” — United Nations, Paris Agreement, December 2015
Part A — Describe
Describe the historical context in which the Paris Agreement was developed.
Tip: Think about the broader environmental and political developments from the late 20th century that made this agreement necessary. What had been happening to the climate, and what earlier efforts had already been made?
Model Response: The Paris Agreement was developed in the context of growing scientific consensus and global alarm about climate change, following decades of accumulating evidence that human greenhouse gas emissions were driving dangerous rises in global temperature. Earlier agreements like the Kyoto Protocol (1997) had attempted to address emissions but were limited in scope and enforcement, particularly as major emitters like the United States and China were not fully bound by its commitments. By 2015, visible effects of climate change—more extreme weather events, rising sea levels, and shrinking glaciers—had increased public pressure on governments to reach a more comprehensive and globally inclusive agreement.
Part B — Explain
Explain ONE way in which environmental changes influenced the content or structure of the Paris Agreement.
Tip: Look at what the agreement specifically prioritizes—temperature limits, forests, emissions timelines. Connect one of these directly to an environmental development from the textbook or video.
Model Response: The widespread deforestation of the 20th century—driven by agricultural expansion, population growth, and industrial demand—directly influenced the Paris Agreement's inclusion of a provision encouraging countries to conserve and restore forests as "sinks" for greenhouse gases. Because forests absorb CO₂, their destruction had accelerated atmospheric warming; the agreement therefore recognized that protecting them was an essential part of any credible climate strategy. This provision reflects how the specific environmental changes documented in Topic 9.3, particularly deforestation, shaped the international community's approach to limiting emissions.
Part C — Explain
Explain the significance of the Paris Agreement in light of the broader environmental debates after 1900.
Tip: Think about what makes this agreement historically significant compared to earlier efforts. How did it try to address the tension between developed and developing nations that had defined climate debates throughout the 20th century?
Model Response: The Paris Agreement is historically significant because it represented the broadest international consensus on climate action since the modern environmental debate began, bringing together 195 nations—including both industrialized and developing countries—around a shared framework. It also acknowledged the central tension in climate debates after 1900: that developing nations would need more time to peak their emissions, reflecting the longstanding argument that wealthy industrialized nations had created the problem over 150 years of fossil fuel use and could not simply demand that poorer nations forego development. While critics argued the agreement lacked binding enforcement, it marked a turning point in global recognition that the shared environmental crisis required collective, planetary-scale solutions.
