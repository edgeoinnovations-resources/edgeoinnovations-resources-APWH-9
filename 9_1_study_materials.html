✎ Recall Practice — Multiple Choice (5 Questions)
Select an answer. Wrong choices are disabled so you can keep trying. When you get it right, the full explanation appears.
1. Which technology did the video describe as the "unsung hero" that unlocked the global economy?
Correct — B. The shipping container standardized cargo transport, making loading and unloading dramatically faster and cheaper. Though seemingly simple, it was the key infrastructure that made modern global trade possible.
2. Alexander Fleming discovered penicillin in 1928 when he noticed that mold was killing bacteria in his lab. What unintended consequence did he himself predict in his Nobel Prize speech?
Correct — C. Fleming warned in his 1945 Nobel Prize acceptance speech that overuse of penicillin would allow bacteria to evolve resistance — creating "superbugs." This prediction has proven tragically accurate and remains a critical global health issue.
3. The Green Revolution is best described as a set of agricultural changes that:
Correct — B. The Green Revolution was a scientific attack on hunger using hybrid grains (later genetic engineering), fertilizers, pesticides, and irrigation. China's grain production rose from ~120 million tons (1950) to over 500 million tons (2010) — feeding the world's booming population on essentially the same land area.
4. Approximately what percentage of global energy still comes from non-renewable fossil fuels?
Correct — D. Approximately 90% of global energy still derives from fossil fuels (coal, oil, natural gas). This statistic underscores the core tension: our modern world was built on a resource that will eventually run out, and whose use creates serious environmental consequences.
5. The approval of the birth control pill in 1960 is historically significant primarily because it:
Correct — C. The pill was a "social revolution in a tiny package." For the first time, women had reliable, easy personal control over reproduction. This opened doors to higher education, careers, and reshaped gender dynamics, family structures, and ultimately the global economy.

DOK 2 Knowledge Application Establish & Explain with Examples
✍ Explain Drill 1 of 2
The AP Explain Skill: Don't just say what happened. Show how or why a relationship exists. Connect cause to effect.

Explain how the Green Revolution both solved and created problems for global food security after 1900.

AP Skill: Explain (cause/effect) · Historical Thinking: Causation

Model Response
The Green Revolution solved the immediate crisis of feeding a rapidly growing global population by using hybrid seeds, chemical fertilizers, pesticides, and modern irrigation to dramatically multiply crop yields on the same amount of land. Because of these methods, countries like China saw grain production rise from roughly 120 million tons in 1950 to over 500 million by 2010, which prevented mass famine. However, the revolution created new problems: the expensive seeds and chemicals pushed small farmers off their land, heavy chemical use degraded soil quality and polluted waterways, and the widespread adoption of a few high-yield crop varieties caused thousands of older seed varieties to disappear, reducing biodiversity. As a result, the world became more food-secure in the short term but more environmentally vulnerable and dependent on industrial agriculture in the long term.
✍ Explain Drill 2 of 2

Explain how advances in communications technology after 1900 contributed to political change in the early 21st century.

AP Skill: Explain (how/why) · Historical Thinking: Causation

Model Response
After 1900, advances in communications technology progressively reduced the cost of sharing information until, by the early 21st century, ordinary individuals could broadcast to millions. The internet — originally a Cold War military project — became a global public platform, and when combined with the mobile phone, it placed the power to create and share information directly in billions of people's hands. This shift contributed to political change by enabling rapid grassroots organization outside of traditional power structures. The most dramatic example was the Arab Spring (2010–2012), in which protesters in Tunisia, Egypt, and other countries used social media platforms to coordinate demonstrations, share images of government violence with the outside world, and sustain pressure on authoritarian governments. The technology did not cause the political grievances, but it dramatically accelerated and amplified the movements built around them.

◎ Contextualization Builder — 3 Pathways
AP Contextualization Tip: Contextualization is NOT just background. You must describe a broader historical development and explain how it connects to the specific topic. Aim for 3–5 sentences.
Pathway 1 The Industrial Revolution as the Foundation for 20th-Century Technology ▼
Stem: Technological advances after 1900 did not emerge in isolation — they built on the transformations set in motion by the Industrial Revolution of the 18th and 19th centuries. Consider: How did industrialization create the infrastructure, scientific culture, and global economic networks that made 20th-century technological leaps possible?

Your Contextualization — Pathway 1:

Model Contextualization — Pathway 1
The technological advances that transformed life after 1900 were made possible by the scientific and economic infrastructure built during the Industrial Revolution of the eighteenth and nineteenth centuries. Industrialization shifted economies from agricultural subsistence to mechanized manufacturing, concentrating capital, labor, and knowledge in ways that funded the universities, laboratories, and factories needed to produce penicillin, synthetic fertilizers, and mass-produced goods. The fossil fuel economy that powered industrial factories also provided the energy foundation on which 20th-century global transportation and communications networks were built. Furthermore, industrial capitalism created a global trading system — including shipping routes and financial instruments — that the shipping container and later the internet would further accelerate. In this way, the double-edged technologies of the 20th century were the direct offspring of the double-edged industrial revolution that preceded them.
Pathway 2 World War II as an Accelerant of Technological Development ▼
Stem: Several technologies covered in this section — including penicillin, nuclear power, and the internet's predecessor ARPANET — were directly developed or accelerated by World War II and Cold War military investment. How did global conflict reshape the trajectory of technological development after 1900?

Your Contextualization — Pathway 2:

Model Contextualization — Pathway 2
World War II served as a massive accelerant for the technological advances that would define life after 1945. The desperate need to prevent soldiers from dying of infected wounds led the Allied governments to fund the mass production of penicillin — a drug that had been discovered in 1928 but barely developed. Similarly, the nuclear weapons programs of the Manhattan Project, though designed for destruction, laid the foundation for nuclear energy and radiation medicine. After the war, Cold War competition between the United States and the Soviet Union drove enormous government investment in science and engineering, including the creation of ARPANET — the military communications network that eventually became the public internet. The global conflict and superpower rivalry of the 20th century thus paradoxically accelerated both the most destructive and the most beneficial technological changes in human history, illustrating how war and geopolitical pressure have consistently shaped the pace and direction of innovation.
Pathway 3 Population Growth and the Demand for New Solutions ▼
Stem: The world's population roughly quadrupled over the 20th century, from ~1.6 billion in 1900 to over 6 billion by 2000. This explosive growth created unprecedented demand for food, energy, medicine, and communication — and was itself partly caused by the technologies designed to meet that demand. How does population growth connect to the technological story of this era?

Your Contextualization — Pathway 3:

Model Contextualization — Pathway 3
The explosive growth of the global population over the 20th century created urgent demand for the very technologies covered in this section, while simultaneously being shaped by them. In 1900, the world held approximately 1.6 billion people; by 2000, that number exceeded six billion — a change driven in part by the medical advances (vaccines, antibiotics) and agricultural breakthroughs (Green Revolution) that dramatically reduced death rates. This created a paradox: the technologies that saved lives contributed to a population growth that then demanded even more technological solutions for food and energy. Norman Borlaug himself acknowledged this cycle when he called the Green Revolution a "temporary success, a breathing space" — recognizing that without addressing population dynamics alongside agricultural production, hunger would eventually return. The birth control pill introduced yet another dimension, allowing individuals — especially women — to manage reproduction and thus participate in reshaping the demographic pressures that had driven so much of the century's technological development.
